# Q12. Evaluation methodologies for the distinctiveness / informativeness of LLM-generated explanations — reference-free metrics, n-gram novelty vs. training-corpus platitudes, human-judge protocols.

Several families of evaluation methodologies now target the **distinctiveness and informativeness** of LLM‑generated explanations, combining **reference‑free automatic metrics**, **n‑gram novelty / training‑corpus analysis**, and **human‑judge protocols**. Below is a concise overview tied to your three axes.[^1_1][^1_2][^1_3]

***

### Reference‑free informativeness metrics

Reference‑free metrics assess explanation quality without relying on a ground‑truth reference, making them suitable for open‑ended explanations. Recent approaches include:[^1_4][^1_3]

- **LLM‑as‑judge metrics**: Frameworks such as G‑Eval or OpeNLGauge (OPENLGAUGE) prompt an LLM to score generated explanations on custom criteria (e.g., coherence, faithfulness, helpfulness) and often return explicit rationales, effectively acting as reference‑free evaluators.[^1_5][^1_6][^1_7]
- **Prompt‑based or rulebased metrics**: Some benchmarks define “truthfulness” and “helpfulness” via n‑gram overlaps and hand‑crafted rules instead of human judges, yielding scores that correlate with human‑rated or LLM‑based judgments.[^1_8]
- **Semantic‑uniqueness measures**: Novel methods in NLG evaluation focus on *semantic* distinctiveness (e.g., whether a generated explanation conveys new meaning not captured by other candidate outputs) rather than just surface‑level novelty.[^1_1]

In practice, these metrics are often aggregated into composite scores (e.g., Fluency–Truthfulness–Helpfulness) and tuned so that they correlate with human or high‑quality LLM assessments.[^1_3][^1_8]

***

### n‑gram novelty vs. training‑corpus “platitudes”

To measure **distinctiveness** against training‑corpus habits, researchers analyze n‑gram novelty with respect to the model’s pretraining data:[^1_9][^1_2]

- **n‑gram novelty rates**: Work such as “Evaluating n‑Gram Novelty of Language Models Using Rusty‑DAWG” defines *novelty* as the proportion of n‑grams ( $1 \leq n \leq 100$) in generated text that do not appear in the pretraining corpus. This reveals how much the model tends to reproduce or recombine memorized patterns instead of producing structurally new phrases.[^1_2]
- **Training‑data “platitudes”**: If many n‑grams in explanations closely match high‑frequency training‑data phrases, the model is seen as over‑relying on “platitudes” (generic, boiler‑plate expressions) rather than generating situation‑specific reasoning. Analyses of loss landscapes show that models assign lower next‑token loss to n‑grams that appear in training, encouraging such repetition.[^1_10][^1_2][^1_1]
- **Size vs. novelty trade‑off**: Larger models tend to generate *less* n‑gram‑novel text, indicating greater memorization of training‑corpus patterns, while smaller models or constrained decoding also shape novelty.[^1_9][^1_2]

Used together, such n‑gram statistics help operationalize the idea that **distinctive explanations** should contain relatively many n‑grams that are rare or absent in the training corpus, while still being coherent and factually grounded.[^1_2][^1_1]

***

### Human‑judge protocols for distinctiveness and informativeness

Where automatic metrics are insufficient, carefully designed **human‑evaluation protocols** are used to judge distinctiveness and informativeness:[^1_11][^1_12][^1_13]

- **Dimension‑based rating scales**: Studies often decompose “good explanations” into human‑interpretable dimensions such as:
    - *Selectivity* (only relevant factors mentioned),
    - *Illustrative elements* (examples, analogies),
    - *Objectivity* (minimal bias or misleading claims),
    - *Understandability* and *helpfulness*.[^1_12][^1_13][^1_11]
Participants rate each explanation on these scales on a Likert‑style scale, sometimes comparing LLM‑generated vs. baseline or human‑authored explanations.
- **Comparative or forced‑choice tests**: Protocols may ask judges to choose the more informative, less generic, or more distinctive explanation between two variants, reducing variance and focusing on relative quality.[^1_13][^1_12]
- **Structured annotation schemes**: Some protocols supply explicit annotation guidelines (e.g., “Does the explanation list contributing factors?” or “Does it include illustrative examples?”) to ensure consistent judgments across raters.[^1_11]

These human‑judged scores are then often used as **gold‑standard targets** for training or validating reference‑free metrics and to detect whether LLM‑generated explanations have become distinguishable from generic, training‑corpus‑derived platitudes.[^1_3][^1_11][^1_1]
