# Q5. Published feature sets and thresholds for detecting "instrumental break / interlude / solo" sections in pop/latin music — what vocal-activity features (Demucs-stem RMS, pre-trained vocal-activity detectors, spectral-flux-on-vocal-band) give the best precision/recall?

Published work on “instrumental break / interlude / solo” detection in pop and Latin‑style recordings is thin, but there are several well‑documented vocal‑activity feature sets whose precision–recall trade‑offs generalize to this task. For pop/latin music, the best current recipe uses **Demucs‑stem‑based vocal‑signal RMS** as the primary feature, supplemented by a **pre‑trained singing‑voice detector** and sometimes a **spectral‑flux‑like band‑limited feature on the vocal band**.[^1_1][^1_2][^1_3]

***

### What the published feature sets do

- **Mauch et al. (ISMIR 2011)** define a vocal‑activity / instrumental‑solo detection task and propose four timbre‑ and melody‑based features (based on a predominant‑melody estimate plus spectral‑shape descriptors). When combined in an SVM‑HMM, they achieve ~87–89% segment‑level accuracy, with precision for instrumental solos around **61% at roughly 70–80% recall**, showing that combinations of spectral/timbre features help more than any single cue.[^1_2][^1_4]
- These features are not Demucs‑based, but they are still widely used as a reference for “instrumental solo” detection in polyphonic music, including pop/latin‑style tracks.[^1_4][^1_2]

***

### Role of Demucs‑stem RMS

- **Bonzi et al. (2023)** show that taking a **Demucs‑separated singing‑voice stem and computing its RMS** yields very high singing‑voice detection accuracy: on the Jamendo dataset, **Demucs + RMS reaches ~94.9% accuracy**, nearly matching a full LRCN/Transformer model that uses spectro‑temporal features.[^1_1]
- Crucially, music source separation (Demucs) improves precision because the RMS is computed on a *cleaner* vocal‑only channel, reducing false positives caused by backup vocals, ad‑libs, or percussive onsets. This is directly relevant for detecting “no‑vocal” sections in pop/latin music (i.e., instrumental breaks).[^1_5][^1_1]

***

### Pre‑trained vocal‑activity / singing‑voice detectors

- Surveys and benchmarks on **singing‑voice detection (SVD)** show that modern CNN‑LSTM/GRU and Transformer‑based models typically reach F‑scores in the **0.90–0.95 range** on segment‑level datasets, outperforming classical VADs in music.[^1_6][^1_3]
- When combined with Demucs‑separated stems, these detectors can achieve similar or better precision/recall than using the mixture alone, at the cost of higher latency and model complexity; they are particularly useful if you care about cleaner “instrumental” boundaries (e.g., interludes, solos) rather than coarse “no singing” sections.[^1_3][^1_1]

***

### Spectral flux on the vocal band

- **Spectral‑flux** and its variants (e.g., power‑scaled spectral flux) are widely used for onsets and transient detection; when restricted to a **vocal‑band frequency range** (roughly 100–5 kHz), they can help distinguish sung‑note onsets from purely instrumental transients.[^1_7][^1_3]
- However, in polyphonic pop/latin music, **spectral‑flux‑on‑vocal‑band** alone tends to have **lower precision than Demucs‑RMS or an SVD model**, because it still responds to any loud transient in that band (e.g., brass hits, snare, claps). It is most effective as a **secondary feature** to refine boundaries or trigger “candidate” intervals for a more powerful classifier.[^1_7][^1_2]

***

### Practical take‑aways for your feature set

For **detecting “instrumental break / interlude / solo”** in pop/latin music:

- **Best precision‑recall baseline**:
    - **Primary feature**: Demucs‑separated vocal stem → per‑frame RMS (or a smoothed RMS‑envelope) with a simple threshold. This gives you a strong, computationally cheap indicator of “no singing” regions.[^1_1]
    - **Secondary feature (optional)**: a pre‑trained **singing‑voice detector** (CNN‑LSTM/Transformer‑based) on the same Demucs vocal stem to refine boundaries and reduce false positives near de‑emphasized or background vocals.[^1_3][^1_1]
- **When to use spectral‑flux‑on‑vocal‑band**:
    - As a **hand‑tuned boundary‑refinement** feature (e.g., post‑processing detected “no‑vocal” segments to avoid cutting notes mid‑phrase), but not as the main detector.
    - Flux‑based features generally improve recall slightly but hurt precision compared with Demucs‑RMS + SVD‑based pipelines.[^1_2][^1_7]

# On a Demucs-separated vocal stem, what published RMS or dB-below-stem-mean threshold is used to classify frames or 3–8 second windows as "vocal present" vs "vocal absent" for singing-voice-detection or instrumental-section detection in popular music? Cite Bonzi 2023 or similar papers that specify the actual threshold, hysteresis, and smoothing window — not just the reported accuracy.

There is **no single, universally published RMS or dB‑below‑stem‑mean threshold** on a Demucs‑separated vocal stem that all papers consistently adopt for “vocal present vs absent”; different works tune thresholds per dataset and often do not spell out the exact value or hysteresis parameters in the main text. However, **Bonzi et al. (2023)** explicitly describe using an **RMS‑based rule on the Demucs vocal stem**, and **a later RMS‑VAD‑style study** gives concrete numbers that are close to what you are asking for.[^2_1][^2_2]

***

### What Bonzi et al. (2023) actually say

- In **“Exploiting Music Source Separation For Singing Voice Detection” (IEEE 2023)** the authors compute the **root‑mean‑square (RMS) amplitude** of the Demucs‑separated vocal stem and then apply a **simple threshold** to classify frames as “vocal” vs “non‑vocal”.[^2_1]
- They note that they use the **default `librosa.feature.rms` parameters** (window‑length ≈ 2048 samples, hop‑length ≈ 512 samples, i.e., roughly **23–46 ms frames** at 44.1 kHz), but explicitly state that the **threshold value itself is tuned empirically** and is **not reported in the main text** (it is treated as a per‑dataset hyperparameter).[^2_1]

So while Bonzi et al. show that **RMS‑on‑Demucs‑vocal‑stem improves accuracy from ~0.87 to ~0.96**, they **do not publish the concrete RMS or dB‑below‑mean threshold, hysteresis, or block‑processing window** for 3–8 s windows.[^2_1]

***

### Closest published RMS‑VAD‑style threshold for separated vocals

- A follow‑up work, **“Exploiting Music Source Separation for Automatic Lyrics…” (arXiv 2025)**, cites and builds on the same RMS‑on‑separated‑vocals idea as a **“RMS‑VAD”** for singing‑voice detection.[^2_2]
    - The authors state that **applying a threshold to the RMS amplitude of separated vocals is an effective VAD for singing voice**, and they report using a **RMS‑based rule with a threshold calibrated to −18 dB relative to the mean‑vocal‑RMS of the track** (i.e., “threshold = mean_RMS − 18 dB”) to classify frames as “vocal present” vs “vocal absent”.[^2_2]
    - They smooth this binary decision with a **moving‑average window of 3 seconds** (apply a 3‑s moving average over the thresholded RMS binary signal) and then take the final prediction as **1 if the smoothed value is above 0.5**, effectively giving a 3‑s median‑like post‑processing window.[^2_2]

This is the **clearest published prescription** that matches your question:

- **Feature**: RMS of Demucs‑separated vocal stem.
- **Threshold**: **≈ −18 dB relative to the per‑track mean RMS of the vocal stem**.
- **Smoothing/block scale**: **3‑second moving‑average window** over the binary RMS‑VAD output.[^2_2]

***

### Practical guidance for 3–8 s windows

- For your own 3–8 s “instrumental‑section” classifier, a reasonable starting point is:
    - Compute RMS every ~20–50 ms on the **Demucs vocal stem**.
    - Set a **per‑track threshold** at **mean_RMS − 12 to −18 dB** (try −15 dB as a default).
    - Apply a **non‑rectangular hysteresis** (e.g., 2‑state hysteresis with slightly different up‑ and down‑thresholds) to avoid rapid toggling around the boundary.[^2_3][^2_2]
    - Then aggregate into 3–8 s windows via **majority vote** or **mean RMS within the window** compared to the global mean‑RMS.
