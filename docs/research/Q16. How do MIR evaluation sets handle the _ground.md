# Q16. How do MIR evaluation sets handle the "ground truth is listener-subjective" problem for section labels and downbeat anchoring? Inter-annotator agreement numbers on SALAMI / Harmonix for the labels closest to ours (intro/break/peak/instrumental).

Most MIR “ground‑truth” structural datasets handle the listener‑subjective nature of section labels and downbeat placement by treating the reference as a *representative* expert annotation rather than a single objective truth, and by quantifying or building around inter‑annotator agreement when possible.[^1_1][^1_2][^1_3]

### How MIR evaluation sets treat subjectivity

- **Multiple annotators and voting**: Sets like SALAMI collect multiple independent annotations (often 2) and derive a “reference” (often via best‑match or majority) rather than assuming one annotator is definitively correct.[^1_4][^1_1]
- **Label‑invariant measures**: For structural segmentation, many MIREX‑style evaluations use boundary‑based scores (e.g., Hit Rate, F‑measure with tolerance windows) that do not require labels to match; or pairwise clustering metrics (Pairwise F, Rand index) that only care about whether two time points are judged in the “same” or “different” segment.[^1_5][^1_6][^1_2]
- **Downbeat anchoring conventions**: Evaluations typically allow a small tolerance window (often ±30–70 ms) around each annotated downbeat, and sometimes explicitly prefer that segment boundaries fall on downbeats, reflecting the assumption that human annotators align sections to the bar grid.[^1_7][^1_3][^1_1]


### Inter‑annotator agreement on SALAMI and Harmonix‑style labels

For labels closest to intro/break/peak/instrumental, work on SALAMI and Harmonix‑style datasets shows:

- **SALAMI boundary and label agreement**:
    - Pairwise F‑measure for large‑scale similarity labels averages about **0.76**; for small‑scale labels about **0.69**.[^1_1]
    - Boundary F‑measure (with 0.5 s tolerance) is about **0.69** at large scale and **0.73** at small scale; rising to about **0.80** when taking the best‑matching pairing between two annotators.[^1_1]
    - These numbers indicate that even between expert annotators, there is substantial disagreement on the exact timing and clustering of sections, especially at fine scales.
- **Harmonix‑style functional labels (intro, break/instrumental, peak‑like sections)**:
    - The Harmonix set gives a popular‑music segmentation corpus with labels including `intro`, `break` / `transition`, `instrumental` / `inst`, `bridge`, and `solo`, among others.[^1_3]
    - The Harmonix paper does not report explicit inter‑annotator agreement numbers (most tracks are annotated by a small group of professional musicians, but the paper focuses on single‑reference baselines).[^1_3]
    - However, existing work on multiple‑annotator segmentation corpora (e.g., SALAMI, SPAM) finds that agreement on functional labels is generally lower than on boundaries, especially for labels like “intro/outro”, “transition”, and “instrumental”, which can be interpreted differently by listeners.[^1_2][^1_4]


### Practical takeaway for your labels

- When you evaluate algorithms using SALAMI‑style or Harmonix‑style labels (intro / break / peak / instrumental), it is reasonable to expect performance metrics that are *comparable* to or slightly *above* the reported inter‑annotator agreement (e.g., pairwise F in the **mid‑70s to low‑80s** for large‑scale labels, and lower for fine‑grained or functional labels).[^1_8][^1_1]
- Many MIR papers treat this as a “soft ceiling”: if your system scores above the best‑case inter‑annotator F‑measure, the dataset itself is not constraining you, and you can argue that the labels are in the same regime of subjectivity as other structural‑segmentation benchmarks.[^1_9][^1_8]
