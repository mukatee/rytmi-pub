# Q14. Features for "mellow / chill / intense" vocal character sub-sectioning on a Demucs vocal stem — spectral centroid, roll-off, RMS envelope, f0 contour stability, vibrato depth. Which of these correlate with listener-perceived emotional intensity in published studies?

Several of the acoustic features you list are robustly tied to listener‑perceived emotional “intensity” (arousal/activation) in vocal studies, but the evidence is stronger for some than for others.[^1_1][^1_2][^1_3][^1_4][^1_5][^1_6][^1_7]

### Strongly supported correlates

- **Spectral centroid**: Higher centroid (brighter, more high‑frequency‑energy‑rich voice) is repeatedly linked to higher perceived arousal and intensity in both speech and singing, with listeners judging brighter timbres as “hotter,” more excited, or more intense.[^1_2][^1_8][^1_9][^1_10][^1_11]
- **Spectral roll‑off / spectral slope**: Manipulations of spectral roll‑off (how quickly energy falls off with frequency) are strongly associated with perceived vocal effort, tension, and activation; flatter/less‑damped spectra (more high‑frequency energy) correlate with higher‑intensity emotion.[^1_4][^1_6][^1_1]
- **RMS envelope (intensity / loudness)**: Time‑varying amplitude (RMS‑like intensity) is a primary driver of perceived emotional intensity; higher average level and more dynamic loudness changes are consistently associated with high‑arousal emotions (anger, joy) versus “mellow/chill” (sadness, tenderness).[^1_12][^1_3][^1_1]


### Partially or context‑dependent correlates

- **f0 contour stability / contour variability**: Studies manipulating F0 contours show that resynthesizing neutral speech with emotional intonation contours shifts perceived emotion, and high‑contour (more variable) F0 is often linked to heightened arousal or “play‑acted” intensity, though this interacts strongly with context and authenticity.[^1_5][^1_7][^1_12]
- **Vibrato depth**: Vibrato extent and regularity are acoustically sensitive to emotion and vocal effort, and vibrato is often treated as a marker of emotional expressiveness; however, its correlation with *intensity* is more nuanced and heavily dependent on musical style and training.[^1_13][^1_6]


### Practical takeaway for your Demucs sub‑section

For a “mellow / chill / intense” vocal character axis, the strongest, most consistently reported correlates with perceived intensity are:

- **Spectral centroid**
- **Spectral roll‑off / spectral slope**
- **RMS envelope (intensity envelope)**

F0 contour variability and vibrato depth can be used as *secondary* modulators, since they interact more with genre, authenticity, and expressiveness than with a simple intensity slider.[^1_3][^1_6][^1_1][^1_2][^1_5]

# For emotional-intensity classification of sung vocals on a separated vocal stem, what published ranges or per-singer/per-track z-score thresholds on spectral centroid, spectral roll-off, and RMS envelope map to "mellow / neutral / intense" perceptual bins? Do validated studies use absolute feature values or per-singer/per-track normalization, and at what window length (phrase-level ≈ 3–8 s)?

No single published study gives fixed, universal “mellow / neutral / intense” ranges or z‑score thresholds for spectral centroid, spectral roll‑off, and RMS envelope on a separated vocal stem; instead, most work uses **per‑track or per‑singer normalization** and trains classifiers on relative patterns over segments of order **0.5–3 s**, not full 3–8 s phrases.[^2_1][^2_2][^2_3][^2_4]

### Are thresholds absolute or normalized?

- **Normalization is the norm**: Most singing‑emotion or vocal‑emotion studies normalize features per speaker or per recording (e.g., mean‑zero, unit‑variance) before feeding them to classifiers; absolute values are highly speaker‑ and genre‑dependent.[^2_2][^2_4][^2_5][^2_1]
- **Per‑track / per‑singer z‑scores**: Several works that rank spectral features for emotion classification either use global statistics over a track or speaker to derive standard‑score‑like scales, rather than fixed Hz‑ or dB‑level bins.[^2_4][^2_1][^2_2]


### Typical ranges and “bins” (conceptual, not strict)

- **Spectral centroid**:
    - In singing, mean centroid typically falls in the **rough ballpark of 1.5–2.5 kHz** for many vowels and styles, with higher values (brighter) associated with louder, more intense, or “pressed” vowels, and lower values (darker) with “neutral” or “mellow” modes.[^2_3][^2_6][^2_4]
    - Rather than hard thresholds, these studies treat centroid as a **continuous gradient**; classifier‑based “intense” vs “mellow” splits are data‑driven, not hand‑thresholded.[^2_1][^2_4]
- **Spectral roll‑off (e.g., 25–90% roll‑off points)**:
    - Roll‑off is often computed on mid‑band energy (e.g., 250–4 kHz) and then aggregated per segment; higher roll‑off frequencies (more high‑freq energy) correlate with increased intensity and vocal effort.[^2_3][^2_4]
    - Again, there are **no published “mellow / neutral / intense” dB‑per‑Hz thresholds**; differences are usually expressed as **relative shifts** within a track or speaker.[^2_4][^2_3]
- **RMS envelope (energy)**:
    - Studies on vocal emotion and effort use **short‑term energy** (0.05–0.5 s analysis) and then aggregate over phrases; “intense” labels are tied to higher mean and more dynamic energy, while “mellow/chill” corresponds to lower average SPL or dB‑level.[^2_7][^2_8][^2_3]
    - No standard absolute dB ranges exist for “mellow / neutral / intense” across singing; instead, **z‑score‑like contrasts** (e.g., track‑mean plus/minus 0.5–1 sd) are used implicitly in machine‑learning models.[^2_2][^2_1][^2_4]


### Window length in the literature

- **Short‑term analysis (frames 20–50 ms)**: Most systems first compute centroid, roll‑off, and RMS on short frames (20–50 ms, 50–75% overlap), then pool statistics (mean, std, slopes) over **segments of about 0.5–3 s** representing notes, syllables, or short phrases.[^2_1][^2_2][^2_4]
- **Phrase‑level (≈3–8 s)**:
    - A few studies that model “agitated” vs “calm” performances describe **tempo‑ and loudness‑variation statistics over longer phrase‑like segments (several seconds)**, but these are still used as **high‑level descriptors**, not to define fixed Hz‑ or dB‑level bins.[^2_8][^2_3]
    - There is **no validated consensus** that 3–8 s bins are “optimal” for a mellow/neutral/intense split; instead, 3–8 s windows serve as **segment pooling resolutions** on top of shorter per‑frame features.[^2_8][^2_3]


### Practical takeaway for your vocal‑stem classifier

- **Use per‑track or per‑singer z‑scores** for spectral centroid, roll‑off, and RMS envelope, then train or heuristic‑threshold your “mellow / neutral / intense” bins on that normalized space.[^2_2][^2_4][^2_1]
- **Work at two levels**:
    - Short frames (20–50 ms) for centroid, roll‑off, RMS.
    - Phrase‑scale windows (1–3 s for “mellow/neutral” vs “intense” regions; optionally 3–8 s for longer‑arc expressive arcs).[^2_3][^2_4][^2_8]

If you describe your feature extractor (LibROSA, Essentia, etc.), a follow‑up can turn this into concrete example ranges (e.g., “mellow = below track‑mean z‑score −0.5 in RMS and centroid, 3–8 s pooled”).
