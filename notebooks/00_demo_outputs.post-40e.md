Kizomba typically operates within a comfortable tempo range of 85–110 BPM in a 4/4 time signature. Rather than relying on a heavy percussive grid, the rhythmic pulse is often carried by the bass line and melodic content, making the rhythm feel felt rather than strictly heard. While a steady underlying pulse exists, you will often encounter syncopation and emphasis on off-beat elements. When on the dance floor, you may encounter various sub-styles like the more traditional Angolan kizomba, which emphasizes a grounded pulse, or Urbankiz, which often features more polished, electronic textures and a contemporary energy. You might also hear Tarraxinha, characterized by a slower, more concentrated rhythmic focus. Use these distinctions in tempo and texture as hints to help you place the specific character of a track as it plays.

## bachata - rhythm_anatomy (genre intro)

```
Bachata is a rhythmic dance genre played in 4/4 time, typically maintaining a tempo between 120 and 135 BPM. The rhythmic foundation is built upon a interplay between melodic content and a distinct percussion pattern, where the pulse is often guided by the bongo. While the underlying beat provides a steady framework, heavy rhythmic emphasis frequently lands on specific syncopated accents that define the genre's feel. Most tracks follow a structural arc that moves from an intro through various main sections and breaks, often building toward a high-energy peak before concluding with an outro.

You will encounter several stylistic variants on the dance floor. Traditional Dominican bachata often features a more prominent, driving percussion texture, whereas modern or sensual bachata may emphasize smoother melodic layers and a more relaxed pulse. Urban bachata tends to incorporate contemporary textures and higher energy. Use these rhythmic characteristics as a guide to help you place the music's character as soon as it begins.
```
